| # | Element | Description | Weight | Site-type notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| O01 | Title tags | Source: sf-titles.csv. Every page must have a unique, descriptive title tag of 50–60 characters. Primary keyword should appear near the start. Check for: (1) missing title tags; (2) duplicate title tags; (3) title tags too short (under 30 characters) or too long (over 60 characters); (4) title tags that do not reflect page content; (5) product pages where title is just the product name without product type. | O03 | H1 structure | Source: sf-h1.csv. Every page must have exactly one H1 heading that clearly describes the page's primary topic and incorporates the target keyword. | O11 | URL structure & slugs | Source: sf-urls.csv. | O15 | Bullet & list formatting | Structured list content is preferred by both users and AI systems over prose-buried information. | O16 | Thin content pages | Pages with fewer than 300 words of unique, meaningful text that do not serve a clear user need. | O17 | Content freshness | PROMPT USER. Check: (1) blog/recipe/article section — last update date; (2) product pages referencing discontinued items or old product lines; (3) FAQ links pointing to discontinued products or dead pages; (4) dated statistics referenced in content. | O18 | External linking strategy | Check: (1) key factual claims are supported by links to credible sources; (2) external links are not raw URLs printed in text — they should have descriptive anchor text; (3) external links open in a new tab with noopener; (4) no external links to low-quality or irrelevant sites. | 2.5 | Higher weight for YMYL sites |
| O19 | Internal & external anchor text quality | Anchor text should be semantically descriptive — not generic. Check: (1) internal links to product/category pages use the full keyword-optimised page name; (2) navigation menu anchor text is descriptive; (3) external links use the source name or topic description as anchor text; (4) no keyword stuffing in anchors. | 5 | — |
| O20 | Structured HTML markup | Check source code for: (1) use of article and section tags for content blocks; (2) p tags for paragraphs (not div); (3) ul, ol, li for lists; (4) table for tabular data (not div grids); (5) figure and figcaption for images with captions; (6) nav for navigation. | 5 | — |
| O21 | At least 1 image per page | Every meaningful page should contain at least one relevant image. Check: (1) product pages have a product image; (2) blog articles have a featured or inline image; (3) category pages have representative imagery; (4) images have descriptive file names and alt text; (5) images are relevant to the page content — not decorative stock photos with no connection to the topic. Pages with no images are less engaging for users and less likely to appear in image search results or rich visual formats in AI responses. | 2.5 | Higher weight for e-commerce and recipe/food sites |
| O22 | Keyword strategy | PROMPT USER. The site should have a clear, documented keyword strategy where each page targets a specific primary keyword and supporting secondary keywords. Check: (1) each key page (homepage, product, category, blog) has a clearly identifiable primary keyword that the page is optimised around; (2) the primary keyword appears in the title tag, H1, and first paragraph; (3) there is no significant keyword cannibalisation — multiple pages competing for the same primary keyword; (4) keyword targeting reflects how users actually search, not internal product terminology. | 5 | — |
| O23 | Keyword density | Body content keyword density should be natural and within acceptable range. Check: (1) primary keyword density is approximately 1–2% of total word count — enough to signal relevance without appearing over-optimised; (2) keyword usage feels natural in context — not forced or repetitive; (3) no evidence of keyword stuffing (the same keyword repeated excessively in headings, alt text, or body copy); (4) LSI (semantically related) keywords are present — content uses natural variations and synonyms of the primary keyword. Overly dense keyword usage is a quality signal risk; keyword-absent pages cannot rank for their target terms. | 5 | — |
| O24 | Hidden content | Content hidden from users but visible to search engines — or content hidden from both users and search engines through deceptive techniques — is a violation of Google's guidelines. Check: (1) no text hidden via CSS (white text on white background, font-size: 0, display: none, visibility: hidden) that is present in the HTML for crawlers but invisible to users; (2) no cloaking — showing different content to Googlebot vs human visitors; (3) no doorway pages — pages designed only for search engines with no user value; (4) hidden content in legitimate accordions or tabs is acceptable as long as the same content is served to bots and users. Source: spot-check source code on key pages. | 7.5 | — |
| O25 | Spammy comments | PROMPT USER. Open comment sections on blogs, articles, or product pages that are not properly moderated attract spam — low-quality links, keyword-stuffed text, and irrelevant or harmful content injected by bots. Check: (1) comment sections on blog posts and articles — are there visible spam comments (unrelated links, gibberish text, foreign-language keyword spam); (2) if a comment section exists: is it moderated, and is there bot protection (CAPTCHA or third-party comment system); (3) spam comments already published should be deleted — they dilute page quality signals and can introduce toxic outbound links. | 5 | Higher weight for sites with active blog/article sections and open comment functionality |
| O26 | Google-restricted content | Pages must not contain content that violates Google's search guidelines or that Google specifically restricts from appearing in search results. Check: (1) no content promoting counterfeit goods, dangerous products, or deceptive practices; (2) no automatically generated low-quality content (AI-spun or templated text with no editorial value); (3) no doorway pages or thin affiliate pages with no original content; (4) no scraped content reproduced verbatim from other sources without added value; (5) no malware, phishing elements, or deceptive download prompts. Also check: does the site appear fully in a site:domain.com Google search — a dramatically lower-than-expected page count can indicate a partial manual action filtering restricted content. | 7.5 | — |

| F02 | Backlink profile quality | Report: (1) total backlinks; (2) unique referring domains; (3) percentage dofollow vs nofollow; (4) distribution of linking domain authority; (5) top linking domains. 1000 links from 1 domain is far less valuable than 100 links from 100 different domains. | 10 | — |

| F04 | Anchor text diversity | A natural backlink profile contains a mix of: branded anchors, generic anchors, naked URL anchors, and topical/keyword anchors. ## PHASE 6 — GEO / AI VISIBILITY (36 elements)

Required data: Manual site review, se-ranking-aio.csv, se-ranking-aivisibility.csv, AI Overview spot-checks via WebSearch/WebFetch, social media review

GEO signals evolve rapidly. Elements marked [EVOLVING] reflect current best practices as of Q1 2026 and should be reviewed quarterly.

**Phase 6 Scoring Methodology**
- Score per element: same scale as all phases — Not Present (1) | Weak (2) | Medium (3) | High (4) | Excellent (5)
- Weight: same scale as all phases — 2.5 = Secondary | 5 = Standard | 7.5 = Important | 10 = Critical
- Priority Score (per row): Score × Weight — consistent with all other phases
- Phase GEO Score: sum(Score × Weight) / sum(5 × Weight) × 100 — report this percentage at Phase 6 end.
- Importance Tier labels: 10 = Critical | 7.5 = Important | 5 = Standard | 2.5 = Secondary

| G33 | Clickable article table of contents | Long-form content pages above 1,500 words should include an internal navigation table of contents. LLMs extract section-level content more reliably when sections are clearly delineated. Check: (1) long articles and guides have a clickable TOC near the top of the page; (2) TOC uses anchor links pointing to named heading IDs; (3) TOC reflects the H2 and H3 structure of the page; (4) on mobile, TOC is collapsible or sticky to avoid blocking content. | 5 | Higher weight for publishers, documentation sites, and any site with long-form articles |
| G34 | Standalone extractable sections | LLMs prefer content sections that are independently comprehensible without surrounding context. Check: (1) each H2 section can stand alone as a complete answer to its heading question; (2) sections do not begin with cross-references such as "as mentioned above"; (3) key definitions are stated within the section where they are needed, not only in a separate glossary; (4) numbered steps are complete within their section and not split across multiple headings. | 5 | — |
| G35 | Subject-verb-complement sentence structure | LLMs parse and extract meaning more effectively from clear subject-verb-complement sentence structures than from abstract, passive, or nominalised prose. Check: (1) spot-check 5 key content pages for dominant sentence structure patterns; (2) identify recurring passive constructions such as "it has been shown that" versus "research shows that"; (3) identify noun stacks where a short direct sentence could be used instead; (4) note whether product benefits are stated directly or buried in qualifications. | 5 | — |
| G36 | Schema markup — LLM entity & rich result signals | Source: sf-schema.csv, Schema Sub-Phase output. Assess schema markup from an AI and LLM visibility perspective — separate from the technical implementation check (T27). Check: (1) Organisation schema with sameAs array linking to all active social profiles — this is the primary signal by which LLMs connect a website to its brand entity across the knowledge graph; (2) Product schema on all product pages including sku, gtin14, and offers — enables AI product surfacing and Google Shopping integration; (3) Recipe schema on all recipe pages with complete recipeIngredient and HowToStep arrays — enables LLM recipe extraction and Google recipe card eligibility; (4) page-type-specific @types (AboutPage, ContactPage) in place of generic WebPage — LLMs assign higher confidence to pages with declared @types; (5) BreadcrumbList item URLs present on all ListItems at position 2 and above — reinforces site structure for LLM topical mapping. Note: T24 (SameAs) and T26 (AggregateRating) findings in Technical directly amplify this score — review both before scoring. | 7.5 | Higher weight for CPG, e-commerce, and recipe-heavy sites where Product and Recipe rich results have direct SERP and AI visibility impact. |
